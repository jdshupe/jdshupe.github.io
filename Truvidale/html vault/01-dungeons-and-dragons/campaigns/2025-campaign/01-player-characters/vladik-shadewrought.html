Backstory

The winds scraped across the dry arid ground of the Expanse, taking with them the moisture of any who ventured out. A village, small and huddled against the wilderness, lay at the mercy of the elements. It is here that my story begins—not as you know me now, not as seeker of magic, not as big strong man, but as child.

My father was a blacksmith, and my mother a tanner. We lived simple lives, forging metal and curing hides to barter for what little we needed. I did not yet know that the thirst of my surroundings was mirrored in myself. lizard can not long for the ocean until it has almost drowned and learned to swim

It wasn't till my 10th year that I had to learn to swim

The Night of the Shadows

In Expanse, we don't get winter the same as rest of world, we get storms that make Brass Dragon wish it had home. That year was worse than any in memory, sandstorms that lasted for days and buried homes, lightning that turned whole areas of desert to glass. Worse still were the things that came with the storm—shadows, unnatural and hungry, stalking the village at night. No one saw them clearly, but people whispered of dark shapes that moved like mist and took whatever they could carry.

It was a moonless night, and the village was silent, save for the howling wind. I lay awake, staring at the ceiling of my family’s small home, when e heard it—the sound of something scraping against the walls. I sat up, heart pounding, and saw a shadow move across the room. It was too dark to make out details, but the figure was large, hunched, and inhuman.

It lunged.

I shouted, throwing himself out of bed as the creature descended on me. I scrambled to the forge and grabbed the last item my father worked on, my axe. I swung wildly, connecting with something solid, but it wasn’t enough. The creature knocked me to the ground, pinning me beneath its weight.

In that moment, as I stared into the creature’s empty, shadowed face, something inside him snapped.

He screamed—not in fear, but in rage—and reached for anyone, anything, any power, to save myself. The weave answered. Ice erupted from my hands, coating the creature and flinging it across the room. I didn’t stop, the weave would not let me. I rose to my feet, hands outstretched, and let the weave flow from me. Ice spread across the floor, and the air grew cold and calm despite the storms. The creature hissed and shrank back, then melted into the darkness.

We did not find my parents, I never did figure out if they were taken, eaten or.... either way, my brethren made their decision. The weave was so much, too much. It flowed through me and I felt connected to it. It was then I made my decision, I would do anything for that connection.

Forging the Name

The shadows never returned, but the village was changed. People whispered about what had happened that night, about the boy who had wielded magic against the dark. Some called him blessed, others cursed, for magic of that magnitude was unheard of in the frontier.

I didn’t care what the villagers thought. I cared only about one thing: the power I had felt that night. It had been raw, untamed, but it was something I welcomed. And yet, I didn’t understand it. I didn’t know where it had come from or how to control it. That ignorance burned at me. I wanted—needed—to know more.

I left the village soon after, setting out to study magic. I wandered from town to town, seeking knowledge wherever I could find it. But magic was rare, and those who possessed it guarded their secrets jealously. I found scraps—old tomes, half-finished spells, and crude enchantments—but it wasn’t enough.

My name, however, began to spread. In one town, a merchant dubbed him “Shadewrought” after hearing the story of his first battle with the shadows. The name stuck, a testament to Vladik’s power and the night that had changed his life forever.

The Quest for Power

Over time, my frustration grew. This world’s magic seems so limited, confined to trinkets and petty charms. I know there is more—I have felt it, we have seen it! My pursuit consumed me. I tested the weave in ways that it had not been before. I tried to emulate magic seen in nature even at the cost of my own health.

My early experiments led to the weave itself—the invisible threads that connected all magic. I began to see magic not as a gift or a skill, but as a force of nature, like wind or fire. And like any force, it can be controlled, if one was strong enough to grasp it.

This belief drove him forward, creating man you see today: a hardened, gruff, and fiercely intelligent seeker of power. I believe that magic is the key to understanding the world, and I am determined to claim it for myself—no matter the cost.

Why He Quests for Magic

Vladik’s drive comes from a simple truth: he believes magic is humanity’s only way forward. The world is harsh, cold, and unforgiving. Magic is the only thing powerful enough to shape it, to bend it to humanity’s will. But the people of this world squander magic, treating it as a novelty or a tool for convenience. Vladik sees himself as different. He doesn’t just want to use magic—he wants to master it.

To Vladik, magic is not just power; it is freedom. And he will stop at nothing to claim it.

The Call to Cinderwind

The tavern was a ramshackle affair, one of many dotting the cold, unforgiving frontier. It wasn’t much, but it had what Vladik needed: a roaring fire, dark corners to sit unnoticed, and plenty of ale. He leaned back in his seat, his fur-lined coat shedding stray snowflakes onto the splintered bench. The evening had been uneventful—just another night watching the ignorant and the drunk babble on about their mundane, magic-lacking lives.

Magic was everywhere, yes, but only the shallowest, most surface-level kind. Trinkets to light hearths, charms to fix broken wagon wheels, and the occasional enchanted sword for the village lord's show of power. To Vladik, this wasn’t magic. Magic was something greater—something deeper, untamed, and unending. He had seen glimpses of it in his years of study, in his experiments with the weave. He’d felt it in the way frost crackled at his fingertips or the way the winds shifted with his words. There was more.

But no one else seemed to care. People were satisfied with their little baubles, like children playing with toys. Bah, Vladik thought, swirling the last dregs of his drink. His people had called him mad for pursuing it, for pushing magic beyond the boundaries of what everyone said it could do. But to Vladik, there were no boundaries.

That’s when he heard it—just a few words, but enough to change everything.

"...just finished my orientation...you wouldn't believe the magic they have there!..."

Vladik’s ear twitched, and his hand froze mid-motion. He strained to hear over the din of the tavern.

"...not sure where he gets them from..."

Who? Vladik wondered. What magic? He gripped the edge of the table, knuckles whitening.

"...can't wait to start at Cinderwind..."

Cinderwind. A name that meant nothing to him, yet it stirred something deep inside. He turned to look at the speaker: a young man, clearly out of his element, talking too loudly to a companion. They were freshly back from somewhere, with eyes wide like children who’d seen a dragon for the first time.

Vladik waited, watching them intently, until the conversation drifted to other topics. He left the tavern quietly, but his mind was ablaze. Cinderwind—what kind of place could this be? The way they spoke of it, this was no ordinary school of magic. It sounded like they had access to magic beyond the mundane trinkets of the world.

For days, Vladik searched for information. He visited traders, questioned wanderers, even spoke to one or two hedge mages who claimed to know something of the school. It was an institute—an exclusive one, not just for wizards, but for anyone with the drive to master their craft. Cinderwind College, they called it, a place promising to make its students the best of the best. And, more importantly, they claimed to have magic—real magic, the kind Vladik had always hungered for.

But not just anyone could join. There was an application process, rigorous and secretive. Vladik was undeterred. Over the next weeks, he worked tirelessly to forge a path to the school.

He reached out to contacts—blacksmiths, merchants, and scribes he’d helped over the years. Through charm, coercion, and sheer determination, he pieced together what he needed: an application form, a recommendation from a dubious alchemist, and a sample of his own work.

The wait was agonizing. Every day, he woke and checked for a messenger, a sign, anything to confirm his acceptance. He worked harder than ever during this time, perfecting his magic and studying the weave. He needed to be ready for whatever secrets Cinderwind held.

Then one morning, it arrived—a letter bearing the seal of Cinderwind College. Vladik’s hands trembled as he broke it open. The letter was brief, congratulating him on his acceptance and providing instructions for the journey to the school.

Vladik smirked. “So... they have let wolf into sheep’s den,” he muttered.
